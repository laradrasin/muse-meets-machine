# Dev Log 001: Building Inside AI Tools

I started Muse Meets Machine because I did not want to write about AI tools only from the outside.

It is easy to have opinions about how AI is changing communication. It is harder, and more useful, to build inside the systems and notice what actually changes: what becomes easier, what becomes more brittle, what has to be named clearly, what breaks when context is missing, and what still requires human judgment.

This dev log is part of that learning process.

## The shift I keep noticing

The more I use AI tools seriously, the less I think of them as “writing tools.”

They are closer to working environments.

A prompt is not just a request for output. It is an interface. A project instruction is not just a note to the model. It is a form of operational design. A README is not just documentation. It is a translation layer between a private working system and a public reader who needs to understand why the thing exists.

That matters for communication work.

A lot of communications strategy has historically been organized around finished language: the tagline, the pitch, the campaign line, the executive quote, the op-ed, the launch post. Those still matter. But AI-assisted work pushes the communication problem upstream.

Before the final language exists, someone has to decide:

- what context the system needs
- what the system should ignore
- what judgment should stay human
- what can be automated
- what should be documented
- where the source of truth lives
- how the work can be repeated without becoming generic

That is communication strategy, but it does not always look like copywriting.

## Why GitHub became part of the work

Using GitHub has changed how I think about public proof-of-work.

I am not presenting this as a software engineering portfolio. I am using it because versioning, documentation, repositories, deployment, and public project structure are now part of the environment in which communication work increasingly happens.

For someone coming from brand narrative and editorial strategy, that shift is useful.

GitHub makes certain questions unavoidable:

- What is this project?
- What does it do?
- What is public, and what should remain private?
- What would someone need to understand before contributing, evaluating, or trusting it?
- What is the cleanest explanation of the work?
- What has actually been built?

Those are brand questions as much as technical ones.

## What I have been building

My private working systems include an AI operating system that connects Claude projects, Notion, Google Drive, Google Calendar, Gmail, Canva, and recurring project contexts into one adaptive workflow layer.

That system is not public because it contains personal workflow architecture and connected-account details. But the design questions are public enough to study:

- How do you keep an AI system oriented across multiple projects?
- How do you decide when to fetch context and when not to?
- How do you avoid re-explaining yourself in every session?
- How do you make documentation useful without turning it into clutter?
- How do you keep human judgment in charge of routing, prioritization, and interpretation?

I have also been building smaller tools and methodologies: a coding curriculum, deployed learning tools, a product evaluation rubric, a build-log system, and a voice calibration method for AI-assisted writing.

The common thread is not “AI can do everything.”

The common thread is: AI makes the hidden structure of work more visible.

## What this is teaching me about communication

AI tools reward clarity, but not shallow clarity.

They force a person or organization to make implicit things explicit: goals, constraints, audience, voice, priorities, decision rules, examples, edge cases, and definitions of done.

That is why I am interested in context engineering. Not as a buzzword, but as a practical communication problem.

A model can only work with the context it has. A team can only scale a narrative if the narrative is clear enough to travel. A brand can only stay coherent across tools, teams, campaigns, and audiences if someone has designed the underlying system with care.

That is the territory I am exploring here: the place where language, tools, systems, and human judgment meet.

## What I am not trying to do

I am not trying to turn myself into a full-time developer.

I am trying to build enough fluency to understand the environments where AI-era communication is happening: repositories, workflows, docs, automations, deployed tools, and model-mediated interfaces.

That fluency matters because brand and narrative work increasingly has to live inside systems, not just above them.

## Working principle

The best use of AI is not to remove the human from the process.

It is to make the human’s judgment more explicit, more repeatable, and easier to apply in the right places.

That is the premise I am testing, one build at a time.
