# Voice Calibration Methodology

Voice calibration is a method for keeping AI-assisted writing from collapsing into generic polish.

The basic exercise is simple: start with an AI-generated draft, have the human writer revise it, then study the difference between the two versions.

That difference is where the useful information lives.

## The problem

A lot of AI-assisted writing sounds competent and wrong.

Not factually wrong, necessarily. More often, it is wrong in texture. It rounds off the person. It smooths out the friction, specificity, humor, rhythm, hesitation, conviction, and taste that make a voice feel real.

The result is writing that sounds like it could have come from anyone.

For brand narrative, executive voice, founder communication, thought leadership, and personal essays, that is a real problem.

## The method

The method starts with comparison.

1. Generate a draft with AI.
2. Have the human writer revise it until it feels more accurate.
3. Compare the AI draft to the human revision.
4. Look for what changed.
5. Turn those differences into a reusable voice reference.

The goal is not to teach AI a fake personality.

The goal is to make the writer’s real editorial judgment more visible.

## What the comparison reveals

The revision process usually shows patterns.

For example:

- what the writer cuts
- what the writer adds
- where they reject overstatement
- where they add specificity
- how they handle uncertainty
- what kinds of humor they actually use
- what vocabulary feels natural or false
- how much polish is too much polish
- where the AI sounds plausible but not true
- when a draft should be written, and when the system should ask a better question instead

Those patterns become more useful than a generic voice guide because they are grounded in actual edits.

## From voice guide to agent-ready system

A conventional voice guide describes a person’s voice from the outside.

An agent-ready voice system has to go further.

It needs to help the AI make operational decisions:

- What register does this topic require?
- Is humor appropriate here, or would it flatten the stakes?
- Should the system draft, or should it prompt the human for a real reaction?
- Which parts of the piece are scaffoldable?
- Which parts require lived experience, judgment, taste, or timing?
- Where should the human remain visibly in control?

This is where voice calibration becomes more than tone matching.

It becomes editorial infrastructure.

## Why this matters

Most voice guides describe the desired voice in abstract terms:

- smart but accessible
- warm but authoritative
- clear but nuanced
- confident but not arrogant

Those phrases can be directionally useful, but they are too broad to guide real decisions.

Voice calibration works from evidence. It looks at what a person actually does when they improve a draft.

That makes the voice reference more practical. It can guide future drafts, edits, prompts, examples, review standards, and AI-agent behavior.

## What this is not

This is not a prompt hack.

It is not a way to outsource authorship.

It is not about making every sentence sound quirky or “human.”

It is a way to protect judgment inside an AI-assisted workflow.

The human remains responsible for meaning, standards, taste, and final language. The system becomes better because those preferences are no longer implicit.

## Where it fits

Voice calibration is useful anywhere AI is being used to support communication without replacing the person behind it.

That includes:

- founder voice
- executive communication
- brand writing
- newsletter writing
- thought leadership
- social posts
- essays
- internal messaging
- editorial systems
- AI-assisted content workflows

The method is especially useful when someone knows an AI draft is “not quite right” but cannot yet explain why.

## Working principle

A strong AI-assisted writing system should not make the human sound more like the machine.

It should make the human’s judgment easier to recognize, repeat, and protect.
